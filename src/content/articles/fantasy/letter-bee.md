## Story Overview

Amberground is a world of perpetual night. The artificial sun Akatsuki illuminates only the capital; the outer regions exist in darkness broken only by lanterns and the courage of the Letter Bees — government couriers who carry mail through terrain patrolled by Gaichuu, giant armored insects that consume human hearts.

Lag Seeing is a boy who was once himself delivered as a letter. He becomes a Letter Bee and sets out to find Gauche Suede, the Bee who first transported him and then disappeared under mysterious circumstances. The search reveals a conspiracy involving Amberground's government, the nature of the Gaichuu, and what the letters actually carry.

The series uses the concept of "heart" literally — letters contain fragments of the writer's emotional memory, Lag's tears are a weapon that reveals those fragments, and the Gaichuu consume the hearts of those they defeat. This makes every letter delivery a genuinely high-stakes emotional act.

## Characters

## Art Style

Hiroyuki Asada's visual style suits the story it tells. Emotional moments land because facial expressions are drawn with real attention to subtlety — you rarely need dialogue to understand what a character is feeling. Background detail varies by scene, pulling back in quiet moments and getting tight and detailed when the stakes rise.

## What I Love About It

Lag cries constantly, and somehow Asada makes this work. His tears are literally a weapon — they reveal the emotional memories embedded in letters — but they also communicate something true about him: he feels things fully and does not hide it. Growing up, I was told that crying was weakness. Lag's tears save people. I found that meaningful in a way I still find hard to articulate.

The world design is extraordinary. Every environment in eternal night has been considered — how light works, how people live, what makes the darkness beautiful rather than merely threatening. Asada draws shadow the way other artists draw action.
