## Story Overview

Karasumori School is built on supernaturally powerful land. At night, ayakashi are drawn to this power and grow stronger and more dangerous within its influence. The Sumimura and Yukimura families have been its protectors for generations, creating barriers to contain and destroy the creatures.

Yoshimori Sumimura is seventeen and inherits this duty from his grandfather, competing with Tokine Yukimura next door. They are rivals by family tradition and friends by childhood. Yoshimori is powerful but imprecise; Tokine is technically superior despite less raw power. Their dynamic is the series' initial engine.

The story develops beyond nightly ayakashi battles as human organizations seeking to exploit Karasumori's power draw the two families into conflicts with stakes beyond their neighborhood.

## Characters

**Yoshimori** — His quality is a specific combination: enormous raw power and equally enormous reluctance to harm anything. He would rather bake sweets than fight. His development involves learning to direct his power precisely enough to protect the people around him without destroying what he wants to protect.

**Tokine** — Her technical superiority and her careful control are deliberate contrasts to Yoshimori's raw power. Her relationship with him is complicated by family rivalry, childhood history, and a debt she carries from a past incident.

**The supporting cast** — Both families' members are developed as individuals; the supernatural organizations that emerge provide antagonists with their own coherent motivations.

## Art Style

Tanabe's art handles both the supernatural combat and the everyday school-life sequences with consistent quality. The kekkai (barrier) visualizations are creative and distinctive; character designs are clear and expressive across a large cast.

## Cultural Context

Kekkaishi draws on the Japanese tradition of supernatural territorial protection — the idea that specific places require human guardians and that this guardianship passes through family lines. The ayakashi draw from Japanese folklore; the kekkaishi power system is Tanabe's invention but feels grounded in the tradition it references.

## What I Love About It

The chapters where Yoshimori's baking — his chosen escape from the duties he did not choose — becomes relevant to the plot, connecting his private self to his public role in ways he did not plan. The series respects what characters care about when they are not performing their duties.
