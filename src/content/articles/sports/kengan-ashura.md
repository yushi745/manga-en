## Story Overview

The Kengan matches operate according to a simple rule: when two corporations cannot resolve a dispute through normal means, they submit to underground gladiatorial combat. Each company fields a fighter; the winner's employer prevails. The practice is secret, officially illegal, and widely used by Japan's largest corporations.

Kazuo Yamashita, an undistinguished salaryman, witnesses a match and meets Ohma Tokita, who defeats three fighters simultaneously without apparent effort. His employer, intrigued, decides to enter the Kengan Tournament — a once-per-decade competition among all participating corporations to determine the ultimate Kengan fighter and the rights that come with victory.

The series follows Yamashita and Ohma through the tournament, which reveals fighters representing wrestling, boxing, Chinese martial arts, Brazilian jiu-jitsu, karate, judo, and systems that exist only in the Kengan world. Ohma's background — where he came from, what he is, what he seeks — develops across the series.

## Characters

**Ohma Tokita** — His fighting ability exceeds what his background initially explains. As the series develops his history, the gap between his capability and his appearance becomes a plot element rather than a power fantasy. His relationship with Yamashita — fighter and nervous handler — is the series' emotional anchor.

**Kazuo Yamashita** — His function is unusual for this genre: he cannot fight, he has no special ability, and he is exactly as bewildered as any ordinary person would be in this world. His development is entirely about understanding rather than capability, and he becomes the reader's point of entry into a world of impossible fighters.

**The tournament roster** — Each fighter is given enough backstory and personality to matter as a character rather than as an obstacle. Their martial arts systems are explained sufficiently to understand what they are doing and why certain matchups favor certain styles.

## Art Style

Daromeon's art is the series' most impressive quality — the fight sequences are exceptional, with each fighter's style visually distinct and the mechanics of technique legible on the page. The character designs vary enormously across the roster; the same artist draws wrestlers, grapplers, strikers, and hybrid fighters with equal conviction. The action choreography is detailed enough to follow move by move.

## Cultural Context

Kengan Ashura satirizes Japanese corporate culture through its premise — the gladiatorial resolution of business disputes is an absurdist extension of real corporate competition — while taking the combat itself seriously. The series does not ask the reader to find the premise funny; it asks them to accept the world's rules and engage with what those rules produce.

## What I Love About It

The fights where a technically inferior fighter wins by understanding something specific about their opponent's approach — tactical victories rather than power surges — are the series' best content. Sandrovich designs each matchup around a question: what defeats this style, and does this specific fighter know the answer?

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers describe Kengan Ashura as the martial arts manga with the most reread value — the fight sequences reward analysis and subsequent encounters reveal foreshadowing in earlier ones. The finale of the tournament is described as genuinely earned after 27 volumes of setup. The sequel, Kengan Omega, is described as a worthy continuation for readers who want more.
