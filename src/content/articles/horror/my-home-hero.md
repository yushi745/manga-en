## Story Overview

Tetsuo Tosu is a salaryman who reads mystery novels for fun. His daughter Reika has been living with a boyfriend he hasn't met. When Tetsuo finally investigates, he discovers the boyfriend is violent — and connected to organized crime.

One night, Tetsuo acts. He kills the man.

What follows is not a story about whether he was right to do it. It's a story about what happens next: the disposal, the cover-up, the investigation that the dead man's yakuza associates launch, and Tetsuo's desperate use of everything he knows from decades of mystery novels to stay one step ahead of people who do this professionally.

His wife Kasen becomes his partner in the cover-up. His daughter Reika doesn't know what happened.

## Characters

**Tetsuo Tosu** — The series' most interesting element — a man who is simultaneously sympathetic (his motive is protecting his daughter) and in over his head (everything he does is improvised from mystery novel knowledge). His competence when it comes is earned rather than innate.

**Kasen Tosu** — His wife's decision to become his accomplice rather than his confessor is the series' most important character choice, and her own competence and strategic thinking make her arguably the series' most capable character.

**Reika** — Her ignorance of what her parents have done for her is the series' central irony and the source of its emotional weight.

**The yakuza associates** — Antagonists who start as unknowing investigators and gradually close in, creating the series' primary tension.

## Art Style

Tsutsui's art handles the tonal range of the series — ordinary domestic scenes, moments of horror, crime thriller sequences — with consistent competence. The ordinariness of the Tosu family home as the setting for criminal activity creates effective contrast.

## Cultural Context

Japanese salaryman culture's quiet desperation — the man who works hard, provides, and has no agency over his circumstances — is given unexpected teeth here. Tetsuo's mystery novel habit, a standard salaryman hobby, becomes the only tool he has. The series uses the ordinariness of his life as the source of both the horror and the competence.

## What I Love About It

The series doesn't let Tetsuo be secretly heroic or professionally capable. He is making it up as he goes, guided by fictional templates that may not apply, surrounded by people who are much better at this than he is. His survival is contingent on improvisation and his wife, and the series is honest about that.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers describe My Home Hero as one of the most consistently tense seinen crime thrillers currently available in English — specifically praised for its ordinary-protagonist premise, its treatment of the marriage dynamic, and its willingness to make the cover-up genuinely difficult and escalating rather than conveniently resolving.

The chapter where Tetsuo realizes the yakuza associate investigating the disappearance has figured out more than Tetsuo thought — and what Tetsuo does with that realization using only the deductive frameworks from mystery novels — is the series' best demonstration of what makes its protagonist interesting.
