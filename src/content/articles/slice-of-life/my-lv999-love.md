## Story Overview

Aoki likes his classmate Hashimoto. One day he borrows her eraser, which has the name 'Ida' written on it — Hashimoto likes Ida. Ida spots Aoki with the eraser and thinks Aoki wrote his name on it — meaning Aoki likes him. Aoki, too embarrassed to correct the misunderstanding, goes along with it. Then something unexpected happens: the more time he spends with Ida to 'pretend,' the more his feelings start to actually change.

## Characters

Aoki's journey from 'I'm definitely not gay' to 'oh no' is handled with humor and genuine sensitivity. Ida is straightforwardly accepting and kind, his possible feelings for Aoki developing in parallel. Hashimoto remains a friend throughout, and the series doesn't treat the LGBTQ+ elements as scandal or drama.

## Art Style

Aruko's art is clean and expressive — the comedy reactions are well-drawn and the romantic moments are warm. Character designs are distinct and the art handles both funny and sincere moments effectively.

## Cultural Context

My Love Mix-Up! exists in the tradition of BL-adjacent shoujo — manga for women and girls that explores same-sex romance without full BL framing. The Japanese market has a rich tradition of this type of content, and the series is notable for handling it with more nuance than typical.

## What I Love About It

What impressed me about My Love Mix-Up! is that it never treats Aoki's changing feelings as a crisis or a revelation to be feared — they're just feelings, developing naturally, worth paying attention to. The series is kind to its characters about who they are becoming. That kindness feels unusual and valuable.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

My Love Mix-Up! received strong reviews internationally and sold well in VIZ's Shojo Beat imprint. LGBTQ+ readers praise the positive, non-melodramatic handling of the subject matter. The complete 9-volume run is praised as a satisfying arc.

**Spoiler Warning**: The moment when Aoki finally admits — to himself and then to Ida — that his feelings are real and not a misunderstanding, and Ida's response, is the most emotionally satisfying moment in the series.
