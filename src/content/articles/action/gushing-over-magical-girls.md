## Story Overview

Hiiragi Utena has loved magical girls her entire life — the costumes, the poses, the battles. She has never questioned why she finds the villains the magical girls fight so fascinating.

Enorme, a dark organization, recruits her and grants her power. She expected to become a magical girl. What she becomes is Leoparde — a villain whose power defeats and dominates the magical girls she grew up watching.

She is very good at this. She is also, after some adjustment to the discovery, fine with it.

The series follows Leoparde's career as an Enorme villain, her growing understanding of what she actually is, and the dynamics between the villains and the magical girl heroes. The genre awareness is consistent — the series knows exactly which conventions it is subverting.

## Characters

**Hiiragi Utena / Leoparde** — A protagonist whose villain turn is psychologically honest — she was always more interested in the villain's role than she admitted, and becoming Leoparde is the series revealing that to her.

**The Tres Magia** — The magical girl heroines who serve as Leoparde's opponents and, eventually, something more complicated than simple antagonists.

**The Enorme organization** — The villains whose ranks Utena joins; the series treats them with the same character investment as the heroines.

## Art Style

Ononaka's art handles both the magical girl aesthetic (elaborate transformation sequences, costume design) and the M-rated content with technical skill. The visual language of the magical girl genre is used knowingly — the art communicates genre awareness through its own style choices.

## Cultural Context

Gushing over Magical Girls sits in a tradition of magical girl genre deconstructions — Madoka Magica, Magical Girl Site, Magical Girl Raising Project — while being more interested in the genre's internal dynamics than in horror. The dominant-submissive framing draws from specific doujinshi traditions that the official manga makes explicit. This is the series knowing its audience.

## What I Love About It

Utena's self-discovery is genuine. The series doesn't treat her as simply a pervert who became a villain. It treats her as someone who discovers that her love for magical girls always had a specific texture that she hadn't examined, and that the villain role fits her in ways that are psychologically real rather than simply comedic.
