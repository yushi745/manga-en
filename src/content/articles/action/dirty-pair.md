## Story Overview

The World Welfare Works Association (WWWA) employs Trouble Consultants — specialists deployed to solve problems across the known universe. Kei and Yuri, Code Names Lovely Angels, are among the best. Their missions succeed. Their targets are neutralized. Their clients' problems are resolved.

The collateral damage, however.

The Lovely Angels are also known as the Dirty Pair — because their successful missions have a habit of leaving behind destroyed cities, exploded space stations, and catastrophically altered planetary surfaces. They are not responsible. It is not their fault. Things just happen around them.

This combination — genuine competence plus impossible disaster — is the series' comedy engine. Each case deploys Kei and Yuri in a situation requiring their specific skills, and each case resolves correctly while the surrounding environment becomes a write-off.

## Characters

**Kei**: The physical one — hot-tempered, action-first, tends toward direct solutions involving explosions. Her bluntness is both her most effective quality and the source of most of the collateral damage.

**Yuri**: The more romantic one — interested in men, fashion, and the finer things — though this does not make her any less lethal. The contrast with Kei produces the series' central comedy.

**Their dynamic**: The relationship between Kei and Yuri — mutual exasperation, genuine loyalty, absolute trust in crisis — is the series' actual subject. They are different enough to be funny together and similar enough to function as a unit.

## Art Style

Toshiki Yui's art captures the 1980s science fiction aesthetic: elaborate machinery, colorful space settings, character designs with the particular quality of late-Showa Japanese sci-fi illustration. The action sequences are energetic. The fanservice reflects the era's conventions.

## Cultural Context

The Dirty Pair property originated in Haruka Takachiho's light novels (1979), with the manga and anime adaptations following through the 1980s. The Lovely Angels concept — female action specialists who are brilliant at their jobs and surrounded by chaos they don't cause — was genuinely novel in its era and influenced subsequent science fiction that featured female action leads.

American comic writer Adam Warren's 1988-1990 Dark Horse version introduced the characters to Western audiences, creating an independent fanbase that has persisted.

## What I Love About It

I love the specific comedy of the premise: that they are actually very good at their jobs.

This is key. The Dirty Pair's chaos is not incompetence — it is excellence in conditions that the surrounding universe cannot handle. They accomplish every mission objective. The planet's remaining thirty percent is a separate issue.

This structure produces a different kind of comedy than simple incompetence does. There's a satisfaction in watching Kei and Yuri succeed completely, within the rubble of their success. The joke requires them to be excellent; it wouldn't work if they were just clumsy.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Better known in English-speaking markets through the anime adaptations and the Adam Warren Dark Horse comics than through the original Japanese manga. Among classic sci-fi and anime fans, Kei and Yuri are recognized as significant figures in the history of female sci-fi action heroes. The Warren versions have a dedicated Western fanbase.
