## Story Overview

Noragami: Stray Stories collects short stories that take place in the world of Noragami but outside the main series' narrative. Some stories are pure comedy — Yato's various schemes for increasing his shrine's popularity, Yukine's adjustments to being a shinki, Hiyori's navigation of her dual-existence problem. Others extend mythology from the main series.

The content assumes familiarity with the main series and does not introduce the world or characters. It is supplementary material in the honest sense: more of what fans already want rather than an entry point.

## Characters

The full cast of Noragami in side-story situations — Yato, Yukine, Hiyori, Kazuma, Bishamon, and others — each receive short stories that show them in circumstances the main series' more serious narrative does not prioritize.

## Art Style

Adachitoka's art is consistent with the main series — clean, expressive, and appropriate to both the comedic and action-adjacent content in the anthology.

## Cultural Context

Stray Stories material was collected from various supplementary publications alongside the main Noragami run. The short story format reflects the Japanese manga tradition of tankobon bonus content collected into companion volumes.

## What I Love About It

Yato in pure comedy mode. The main series uses Yato's comedy as relief between serious developments; in the shorter format, the comedy is the entire register, and Yato's specific combination of genuine deity power and absolutely undignified behavior is at its funniest without the dramatic weight.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers describe Stray Stories as exactly what it is — essential for fans, not an entry point for new readers. Specifically noted for the comedic stories being genuinely funny rather than padded, for the additional mythology content being useful context, and for Adachitoka's art being as strong in short form as in long form.

## Memorable Scene ⚠️ Spoiler Warning

Any story where Yato attempts something for the specific purpose of increasing his shrine's popularity or his number of worshippers, and the attempt goes in a completely unexpected direction, is the anthology at its most purely enjoyable.
