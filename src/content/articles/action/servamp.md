## Story Overview

Mahiru Shirota has a simple philosophy: if there's a problem in front of you, fix it. He finds a stray cat, takes it home, names it Kuro. When Kuro transforms into a human-form vampire and explains that Mahiru has accidentally contracted with him, Mahiru's response is to figure out how to make the situation work rather than to panic.

Kuro is Sleepy Ash, the Servamp of Sloth. The seven Servamps — each representing one of the deadly sins — are ancient vampires who exist in contract with human Eves, drawing power from them and serving them. But Kuro has been avoiding having an Eve for a century, and his previous relationships with Eves have left deep damage.

The conflict arrives when the Servamp of Greed abandons his Eve and begins targeting the human world, creating a crisis that Mahiru's straightforward approach is unprepared for.

## Characters

**Mahiru Shirota** — A protagonist whose simple moral clarity — "just do the right thing" — is played against a world where the right thing is genuinely complicated.

**Kuro (Sleepy Ash)** — The vampire whose avoidance of everything is protective rather than lazy; his history with previous Eves is the series' most emotionally substantive content.

**The other Servamps** — Each deadly sin generates a different personality and a different type of conflict.

## Art Style

Tanaka's art has a dynamic quality suited to the supernatural action — the Servamps' various combat forms are visually distinctive, the character designs for the seven Servamps communicate their sin's character, and the darker moments are staged with appropriate visual weight.

## Cultural Context

Servamp ran in Monthly Comic Gene from 2011 to 2022. The seven deadly sins structure for supernatural beings has precedent in manga (Seven Deadly Sins among others) but Servamp applies it to the Servamp/Eve contract dynamic in a way that makes each sin relevant to the character's specific relationship to service and power.

## What I Love About It

Kuro's avoidance. He is categorized as Sloth but the avoidance is not laziness — it is self-protection from a history of Eves and what happened to them. The series takes its time revealing what he is actually avoiding, and the reveal makes the comedy of his avoidance retroactively more affecting.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers describe Servamp as a solid supernatural action series with stronger character work than the premise initially suggests — specifically noted for Kuro's emotional depth becoming more apparent in later volumes, for the Servamp/Eve dynamic creating interesting power relationship questions, and for the seven sins structure organizing the cast effectively. Recommended for supernatural action fans.
