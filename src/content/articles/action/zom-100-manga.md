## Story Overview

Akira Tendo worked for three years at a black company — unpaid overtime, abusive management, complete exhaustion. When zombies appear outside his window one morning, his first conscious thought is: he doesn't have to go to work today.

He makes a bucket list: 100 things to do before he becomes a zombie. Eat ramen at his favorite place. Go on an adventure. Help his friends. Live.

The series follows Akira and the companions he gathers — Kencho, his old friend; Shizuka, a practical survival-focused woman; Beatrix, a German woman who came to Japan for her own reasons — as they navigate the zombie apocalypse while actually pursuing the bucket list rather than simply surviving.

The 16 volumes track the bucket list's completion, the companions' development, and a gradual darkening as the apocalypse's human element becomes more dangerous than the zombies.

## Characters

**Akira Tendo** — A protagonist whose overwhelming optimism is specific rather than generic — it comes from three years of having no joy, and the apocalypse giving him permission to want things is genuinely moving alongside being genuinely funny.

**Shizuka Mikazuki** — A female lead whose practical survival focus contrasts with Akira's optimism in ways that develop into genuine connection; her own relationship to the pre-apocalypse world is as meaningful as Akira's.

## Art Style

The art handles the tonal contrast between comedy and gore with skill — the zombie sequences are drawn with appropriate visceral impact, and the comedy sequences are visually energetic. The character designs are appealing and consistent.

## Cultural Context

Zom 100 ran in Monthly Sunday S and was adapted into a 2023 anime. Its cultural resonance with Japanese readers was significant: the black company (ブラック企業) premise is a recognized social phenomenon in Japan, and Akira's relief at the apocalypse was immediately legible as satire to readers who understood what his three years had been. International readers who didn't know the specific terminology still recognized the burnout.

## What I Love About It

The bucket list is genuine. Each item is specific — not generic adventure stuff but things that reflect who Akira is and what three years of exhaustion took from him. When he completes an item, the victory feels real because the lack felt real. The comedy is funny because the satire underneath it is honest.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers consistently describe Zom 100 as the zombie comedy that finally did something new with the genre — specifically noted for the workplace exploitation backstory giving Akira's optimism genuine emotional grounding, for the comedy being consistently funny rather than just dark, and for the ensemble cast being individually developed enough to make the later dramatic turns land. Frequently recommended as the zombie manga for people who are tired of grim survival narratives.
