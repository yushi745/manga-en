## Story Overview

Misao Harada has been able to see spirits since childhood. A boy who appeared to protect her from supernatural threats disappeared when they were young, reappearing now as Kyo Usui — a tengu demon lord who has been watching over her for years.

Misao is the bride of prophecy: eating her flesh grants immortality; drinking her blood confers power; marrying her gives the supernatural clan she belongs to dominance over others. Every supernatural faction wants her. Kyo claims her as his bride and makes protecting her from rivals identical with possessing her.

The 18 volumes follow the escalating supernatural conflict, Misao's navigation of her situation, and the specific nature of a romance where love and control are impossible to fully separate.

## Characters

**Misao Harada** — A protagonist whose situation is more clearly drawn than her agency within it; the series is most interesting when it allows her genuine choices and least interesting when those choices are constrained to the romantic dynamic's terms.

**Kyo Usui** — A love interest whose possessiveness is presented as the consequence of his nature and her value — the series provides in-world logic for his behavior without fully endorsing it.

## Art Style

Sakurakoji's art is elegant and detailed — the demon world designs are elaborate, the romantic scenes are drawn with attention, and the supernatural violence has appropriate visual weight. The art quality is consistent across 18 volumes, which is an achievement in itself.

## Cultural Context

Black Bird ran in Betsucomi from 2006 to 2012 and was a massive bestseller — it outsold Twilight in Japan during its peak period, which gives a sense of its cultural position. The possessive supernatural romance was already a genre convention when Sakurakoji published Black Bird; she executed it with more craft and consistency than most, and the series is therefore the best representative of what the genre does rather than an outlier within it.

## What I Love About It

Sakurakoji commits. She does not hedge the possessive dynamic with romantic-comedy moments or step back from the power imbalance when it becomes uncomfortable. The series has a coherent internal logic about what Kyo is and what that means for Misao, and it maintains that logic across 18 volumes. That consistency is what separates it from lesser examples of the same premise.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers are divided in a way that accurately represents the series' actual content — readers who enjoy dark possessive supernatural romance rate it highly; readers who want balanced power dynamics rate it poorly. Both groups are correct. The series is exactly what it appears to be, executed with genuine craft. It consistently appears on lists of both best and most problematic shojo manga, and those assessments are compatible.
