## Story Overview

Senpai (never named) is a second-year high school boy who uses the deserted art club room to draw manga and be alone. Nagatoro is a first-year girl who discovers him there, reads his work, laughs at it, and begins visiting every day to tease him about everything.

The series follows their daily interactions — Nagatoro teasing, Senpai sometimes accepting it and sometimes pushing back, both of them slowly learning who the other person actually is. Nagatoro's friends join occasionally. Senpai's art improves. The gap between what they say and what they feel gradually narrows.

## Characters

**Nagatoro** — A protagonist whose teasing is affection made visible for everyone except its target — her embarrassment when her feelings become too explicit, and her delight when she gets a reaction from Senpai, make her one of the genre's most expressive characters.

**Senpai** — The development from passive victim to someone who genuinely engages with Nagatoro — who teases back, who notices her, who cares about her opinion of his work — is the series' most satisfying arc and the reason the romance works.

**Nagatoro's friends** — Her social circle provides context for who Nagatoro is outside the art club room, and their growing awareness of what's happening between the two of them creates useful comedic perspective.

## Art Style

774's art is expressive with a specific emphasis on Nagatoro's face — the variety of expressions, from gleeful cruelty to genuine embarrassment to quiet affection, is the series' most technically impressive element. Senpai's reactions carry equal weight in the comedic timing.

## Cultural Context

The teasing-as-romance dynamic has a specific history in Japanese school romance manga — the idea that someone who treats you as a target for teasing is expressing something they can't say directly is a culturally recognizable shorthand. The series engages honestly with what that means rather than simply using it as an excuse for the protagonist to be treated poorly.

## What I Love About It

The moment the series clicked for me was when I realized that Nagatoro's teasing is never actually mean — she never says anything genuinely cruel, never pushes past what Senpai can handle, and is immediately sensitive when she's gone too far. The teasing is affection in its own specific language, and once you hear it that way, the comedy becomes genuinely warm.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers divide roughly on Nagatoro based on how they read the teasing — those who read it as affection find the series warm and funny; those who read it as bullying find it uncomfortable. The consensus among readers who enjoy the genre is that Nagatoro herself is one of the better-characterized protagonists in school romantic comedy, and that Senpai's development is unusually satisfying.

## Memorable Scene ⚠️ Spoiler Warning

The cultural festival arc, where Senpai exhibits his manga work and Nagatoro encounters it alongside strangers who don't know her connection to it — her reaction, which she can't control, makes her feelings visible to herself in a way the teasing never quite had to — is the series' most emotionally precise moment.
