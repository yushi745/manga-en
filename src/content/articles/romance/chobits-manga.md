## Story Overview

In the near future, humanoid computers called Persocoms are ubiquitous — personal assistants, companions, workers. Hideki Motosuwa is a country boy in the city for cram school who cannot afford one.

He finds an abandoned Persocom in the trash. She has no installed programs — no name, no personality, no memories. She learns her first word from him: "chi." He names her Chi.

As Hideki teaches Chi basic functions, she begins developing in ways that don't match how Persocoms are supposed to work. The mystery of what Chi is — and what she is capable of feeling — is the series' central question. The answer involves what "love" means for something built for and around that function.

## Characters

**Chi** — A character whose apparent simplicity is the series' deliberate misdirection; what she is and what she is capable of is revealed carefully across 8 volumes.

**Hideki Motosuwa** — A protagonist whose ordinariness is appropriate — he is an average person who has been given something extraordinary, and his navigation of that is the series' emotional vehicle.

## Art Style

CLAMP's art in Chobits has the characteristic elegance of their work, adapted for the Young Magazine (seinen) context — cleaner and more overtly appealing than their shojo work. The character designs for Chi and the various Persocoms are distinctive.

## Cultural Context

Chobits ran in Young Magazine from 2000 to 2002 — CLAMP's only major seinen serialization. It was enormously commercially successful and introduced CLAMP to a readership that wouldn't have encountered their shojo work. Its questions about AI consciousness and love are now more directly relevant than they were at publication, which gives older readers a specific reason to revisit.

## What I Love About It

The series earns its philosophical question. The other Persocoms in the series — the various ways human characters relate to them, from use to love to exploitation — give the central Chi/Hideki relationship a context that makes the question of what love means between them genuine rather than sentimental. CLAMP is asking something real.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers consistently note that Chobits is more thoughtful than its reputation suggests — specifically noted for the fan service being the series' most visible element from outside but not its most important one, for the philosophical question having a specific and satisfying answer, and for the supporting characters' relationships with Persocoms illuminating the central romance. Recommended with the caveat that the mature content is present and real.
