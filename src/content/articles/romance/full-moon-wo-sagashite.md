## Story Overview

Mitsuki Kouyama is twelve years old. She has malignant throat cancer. She has one year to live. Her tumor makes singing — her greatest love, the thing she promised to pursue for herself and for a boy she cares about who has since disappeared — physically impossible.

Takuto and Meroko are death gods sent to escort her to death. She bargains with them: grant her a temporary healthy sixteen-year-old body so she can audition for a singing career and find the boy she loves. They agree, reluctantly.

The series follows Mitsuki as Full Moon — her performance identity — pursuing her dream with the specific urgency of someone who knows exactly how much time she has. The death gods' own histories involve deaths and loves of their own, and the series weaves their stories with Mitsuki's in ways that give the supernatural elements genuine emotional weight.

## Characters

**Mitsuki Kouyama** — A protagonist whose cheerfulness in the face of her situation is earned rather than performative; Tanemura establishes the cost of her illness and the specificity of what she's losing before showing how she chooses to spend what time she has.

**Takuto** — A death god whose own story intersects with Mitsuki's in ways the series reveals carefully; his development from reluctant escort to genuine ally to something more is handled with patience.

**Meroko** — The other death god, whose emotional openness contrasts with Takuto's guardedness and whose own loss gives the series some of its most affecting scenes.

## Art Style

Tanemura's art in Full Moon is exceptional — this is her most emotionally precise work, and the art serves that precision. The transformation sequences between twelve-year-old Mitsuki and sixteen-year-old Full Moon are visually sharp. The death god character designs have genuine personality.

## Cultural Context

Full Moon Wo Sagashite ran in Ribon from 2002 to 2004. Its treatment of terminal illness in a shojo manga aimed at young readers was unusual for its directness — Tanemura did not soften the premise or provide false reassurance. The series was adapted into a 52-episode anime. It remains a touchstone for readers who encountered it young for the honesty with which it dealt with death.

## What I Love About It

Mitsuki does not accept her situation. She makes a choice about how to spend her limited time, and she makes that choice with full knowledge of what she is trading — every performance as Full Moon is bought with time she will not get back as herself. The series never lets us forget that. The music means more because of the cost.

## What English-Speaking Fans Say

Western readers consistently rank Full Moon Wo Sagashite among the shojo manga most likely to have made them cry — specifically noted for the death gods' backstories being as emotionally developed as Mitsuki's, for the ending being genuinely earned rather than convenient, and for Tanemura handling a premise that could have been manipulative with enough craft to make it devastating in the good sense. Frequently cited as the shojo manga people read as children that made them understand manga could do serious emotional work.
