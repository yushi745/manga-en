I have a soft spot for the kind of book you find by accident. I was digging through a box of old import comics at a shop in Nakano, looking for nothing in particular, when I pulled out a thin trade paperback with four women in hardsuits on the cover. *Bubblegum Crisis: Grand Mal.* I knew the OVA — every Japanese kid who grew up near a rental shop knew those neon Knight Sabers — but I had no idea there was an American comic made from it. I bought it for almost nothing and read the whole thing standing up on the train home. It surprised me. I expected a cheap tie-in. What I got was a real story about a man dying slowly and the four women who get caught in his way.

## Quick Take

- Adam Warren took the *Bubblegum Crisis* OVA and built a tight, self-contained prequel — no homework required, but it rewards fans who know the world.
- The villain, Peter Vashnevskaya, is the heart of it: not a mustache-twirling boss but a broken soldier whose body is killing him.
- Age rating is T (Teen) — gun and mecha violence, and some uncomfortable depictions of seizures and rage, but nothing graphic.

## Story Overview

The setting is Mega-Tokyo, 2031, six years after the Second Great Kanto Earthquake. The city is rebuilding itself around the Genom tower, and the megacorporation Genom is everywhere — its boomers (synthetic cyborgs) do the heavy lifting of the recovery. The story sits about a year before the events of the original OVA, so the Knight Sabers — Sylia, Priss, Linna, and Nene — are still a young, scrappy four-woman crew taking jobs the police can't handle.

The trouble comes from outside. Peter Vashnevskaya is a Russian-American corporate mercenary, a former soldier who was once hired to steal Genom technology. Genom hit back, and their weapons left him with progressive, fatal brain damage — he now suffers from grand mal seizures that throw him into blind, violent, hallucinatory rage. He has come back to Tokyo for revenge, and he is not alone. He brings the Skorpion, a piece of pre-boomer technology: a networked robotic weapon system that can infiltrate and eventually control other computer systems.

The turning point is what the Skorpion digs up. While burrowing through Genom's data, it learns that Genom's whole success was built partly on the boomer research of one man — Sylia Stingray's father. Vashnevskaya's "extraction raid" stops being a simple heist and becomes something that reaches straight into Sylia's buried family history. By the climax, the Knight Sabers are outnumbered and outgunned, fighting for their lives against a swarm of biomechanical war machines while the real tragedy plays out in Vashnevskaya himself — a man whose revenge is racing his own collapsing brain.

## Characters

**Sylia Stingray** is the leader and the quiet center of the book. She runs a lingerie shop called the Silky Doll as a front, but the story keeps pulling toward her past: orphaned young, raised in the shadow of her father's boomer work, herself genetically altered by him as a safeguard against the technology he helped create. Grand Mal makes her family the thing the whole plot turns on, which gives her more weight here than she sometimes gets.

**Priss Asagiri** is the rock singer and the team's combat specialist — reckless, impulsive, with real anger issues, especially toward boomers. She is the one who throws herself at the fight first, and Warren leans into that hot-headedness.

**Nene Romanova** is the youngest, an AD Police communications operator who moonlights as the Knight Sabers' hacker. She is the one in the chair, in the data, the counterweight to the Skorpion's own intrusion into the networks.

**Peter Vashnevskaya** is the one the title is about. "Grand mal" is the name of his disease and the engine of the whole book. He is a living weapon with a fuse that gets shorter every page — a betrayed man whose revenge is real and whose body is betraying him at the same time. He is the rare tie-in villain you end up feeling sorry for.

## What I Love About It

What stayed with me is how the book treats Vashnevskaya's seizures. Most action comics would make a brain disease into a cheap power-up — the guy gets angry, so he gets stronger. Warren does the opposite. The seizures are framed as something happening *to* him, not for him: blinding, hallucinatory, humiliating episodes that he cannot control, the slow inoperable end of a person. The "grand mal" of the title is not a cool name. It is a diagnosis.

That choice changes the whole feel of the fight. When the Knight Sabers finally clash with him and his Skorpion machines, you are not just rooting for the heroes — you are watching a man lose to his own body in real time, and the violence around him feels like the loud part of a much quieter tragedy. I did not expect a 1994 anime tie-in to make me sit with that. It is the thing that turned the book, for me, from a curiosity into something I actually think about. A villain who is doomed before the heroes even arrive is a hard thing to pull off, and Warren mostly does.

## Memorable Scene ⚠️ Spoiler Warning

The part I keep coming back to is the reveal that the Skorpion has uncovered the truth about Sylia's father — that Genom's rise was built in part on his boomer research. It reframes everything. Vashnevskaya's revenge raid, which looked like a stranger's vendetta against a faceless corporation, suddenly runs straight into the most personal corner of Sylia's life. The mercenary tearing through Mega-Tokyo and the calm woman running a lingerie shop turn out to be connected by the same dead man's work.

It is a smart structural move, and it is the moment the prequel earns its place next to the OVA rather than just borrowing its costumes. The final stretch — the Knight Sabers outnumbered against the Skorpion's war machines — lands harder because of it, since the fight is no longer about stopping a criminal but about Sylia confronting where her own armor really came from.

## Pros & Cons

**Pros:**
- A genuinely good villain — Vashnevskaya's dying-man revenge gives the book real emotional weight.
- Self-contained prequel; you can read it cold without the OVA and still follow it.
- Warren's dense, manga-influenced art is full of detail, with fun background cameos (Bean Bandit from *Riding Bean*, the Dirty Pair) for fans to spot. Original designer Kenichi Sonoda even wrote a supportive introduction.

**Cons:**
- It is one short trade — if you fall in love with this version of the Knight Sabers, there is nothing more.
- The storytelling is packed and jumpy, with flashbacks and asides that can demand a reread to fully track.
- The Knight Sabers themselves read a little off-model from the OVA versions, which is either a fresh take or a wrong note depending on how attached you are — this one won't work for everyone.

## Is Bubblegum Crisis: Grand Mal Worth Reading?

If you want a full, sprawling Knight Sabers saga, this isn't it — it's a single 1994 trade. But as a one-shot cyberpunk story with a surprisingly human villain and a clean tie back into Sylia's past, it is well worth the short read, especially for fans of the OVA or of classic Mega-Tokyo cyberpunk.
